The Center Snellville Never Had

A study of community, identity, and placemaking at The Grove at Towne Center in Snellville, Georgia.

Jonathan Bell May 2026 Gwinnett County, GA
Scroll

Background

A city without a center

Suburban communities across the United States have long struggled with one major issue: creating a distinct identity and a walkable gathering place within environments dominated by strip malls, parking lots, and disconnected commercial corridors. Snellville, Georgia, a city of approximately 21,000 residents located northeast of Atlanta, is no exception. For decades, Snellville lacked a true downtown district. There was no centralized area where residents could work, shop, dine, and connect. Instead, much of the city developed around heavily congested roadways lined with commercial developments, chain restaurants, and large parking lots — what many urban planners would describe as a "stroad."

This began to change with the development of The Grove at Towne Center, a $140 million mixed-use project built on eighteen acres in the center of the city. Formed through a public-private partnership between the City of Snellville, Gwinnett County, MidCity Real Estate Partners, and CASTO, the development includes more than 50,000 square feet of retail, restaurant, office, and entertainment space, a luxury residential complex called The Tomlin, and the new Elizabeth H. Williams branch of the Gwinnett Public Library.

"Borrowing principles from other theories, practitioners built the complete community idea bottom-up, as a persuasive rhetorical vehicle for describing planning intentions and justifying higher densities in positive ways."

Grant, Journal of the American Planning Association (2024)

The Grove was intentionally designed to address what Snellville had lacked for decades. But the emergence of a mixed-use town center in a traditionally suburban community raises important questions. Does the development truly create the sense of community and belonging it promises? How do residents perceive its impact on Snellville's identity and economic future? And what role does the public-private partnership model play in shaping those outcomes?

Location

Positioned at the center

The Grove sits at the intersection of Oak Road, Wisteria Drive, North Road, and Clower Street — within walking distance of South Gwinnett High School, Britt Elementary, Snellville City Hall, a public library, churches, office buildings, and Northside Urgent Care.

Primary Research

What I found on a quiet morning

I visited The Grove on a weekday morning. The weather was warm and overcast, and because of the early hour, the space was mostly quiet. Not many people were outside yet, which actually worked in my favor. Without a crowd to focus on, I was able to slow down and pay closer attention to the space itself — the layout, the design choices, and what everything was set up to do.

When there is not much going on, you notice the space more than the people. Everything is clean, modern, nothing looks temporary or cheap. The library and the coworking space beside it are almost entirely glass on the outside — big floor-to-ceiling panels that let light in even on a gray morning. You can see right through to the inside from the sidewalk.

The green lawn in the middle is where everything comes together. It is wide and open with benches and trees around the edges, and what struck me was how the restaurants and shops do not just sit around it but actually connect to it. Stairs off the lawn lead directly up to Parkside at the Grove. The taqueria opens right onto it too. You are not walking from one thing to another — you are moving through one continuous space.

"Mixed-use developments have several benefits for communities, however due to the complexity of these developments, several challenges arise in the planning and development phases."

Metzinger, Frontiers in Built Environment (2021)

A few people were already out on the lawn. A woman reading by herself on a bench. Some moms with their kids at the water fountains — the kind you can run through — and the kids were soaked even though it was barely mid-morning. You do not see that at a strip mall. People were not there to buy something and leave. They were just there.

The food hall is one of the main anchors. The range is real: Mediterranean, Philly cheesesteaks, pizza, a mac and cheese restaurant, a brewery, a butcher shop. My one criticism was the lighting inside. It is too dim. Coming in from the bright outdoor courtyard, the interior feels noticeably flat. Above the food hall, office and retail spaces occupy the upper floors — still filling in, with more restaurants on the way.

Community Voice

Twenty years in Snellville

During my visit I spoke with Kayla, a Snellville resident of twenty years. Her perspective cuts to the heart of what this research is trying to understand.

She described the city before The Grove as boring — with little to do and no real destination nearby. If she wanted food or anything to do, she had to get on Scenic Highway, which she described as a frustrating and congested drive she tried to avoid. The Grove changed that. It gave her somewhere close that she actually wanted to go.

Kayla — Snellville resident, 20 years

Does The Grove feel like a community space or a commercial one?

Community, without hesitation. She pointed to the outdoor areas, the greenery, and the open seating as what made it feel that way. Not the shops — the space itself.

Has it helped Snellville economically?

She believed it had. Businesses opening, foot traffic increasing, money staying in the city rather than going somewhere else.

Did residents have a voice in how this was built?

She said no, not really. She found out The Grove was happening through a news article — not from the city. Council meetings happened and the information was technically available, but it never reached her. That distinction matters: public input was offered, but it was not brought to people like Kayla.

"The PPP option in urban regeneration projects encounters many obstacles related to high investment risk, low financial and social return, and the numerous sources of uncertainty."

Vale de Paula et al., Journal of Urban Planning and Development (2023)

Kayla's answer about the planning process is the most thought-provoking part of this research so far. A development can feel like community and still have been built without the full community at the table. Both of those things can be true at the same time, and that tension is what this project is trying to understand.

Secondary Research

Sources

Complete Community: Planning Theory From Practice

Jill L. Grant  •  Journal of the American Planning Association, vol. 90, no. 2, Apr. 2024, pp. 213–29  •  doi:10.1080/01944363.2023.2207619

Grant examines the "complete community" concept — the planning idea behind developments like The Grove — and argues that planners and city governments have used this language as a political tool to build support for denser, mixed-use development. Her framework is central to analyzing how Snellville has publicly described The Grove and whether that description holds up against what residents actually experience.

Differences in Experiences With the Development of Mixed-Use Projects From 2004 and 2017

Jamie Metzinger  •  Frontiers in Built Environment, vol. 7, art. 734149, 23 Sept. 2021  •  doi:10.3389/fbuil.2021.734149

Metzinger's survey of developers and construction managers documents how the main obstacles to mixed-use development — zoning, financing, community opposition, market demand — shifted between 2004 and 2017. His finding that no universal best-practice model exists helps explain why The Grove took nearly twenty years from early planning discussions to breaking ground in 2021.

Public–Private Partnerships in Urban Regeneration Projects: A Review

Paula Vale de Paula et al.  •  Journal of Urban Planning and Development, vol. 149, no. 1, Mar. 2023  •  doi:10.1061/JUPDDM.UPENG-4144

This systematic literature review surveys research on public-private partnerships in urban redevelopment and identifies recurring problems around long-term accountability, financing risk, and governance. It provides the structural framework for asking who is ultimately responsible for The Grove — and what happens to the community if priorities shift after the development is complete.
